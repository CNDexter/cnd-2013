Bhutanese history goes back further than the 17th century however much of the history is lost, or is unverifiable, due to a lack of sources. Well-documented history of Bhutan begins in the 17th century when a Tibetan lama started caring for the surrounding lands and unified the local warring factions. During his lifetime he prevented other foreigners or Tibetan’s from infringing upon the lands. He also removed other religious ideologies from the area and wrote a code of laws, the Tsa Yig, which he enforced.1 This solidified the idea of--or concept of--Bhutan as a nation state.

After the lama’s death Bhutan went through a 200-year period of civil war. Though having no stable government or monarch during this time Bhutan was not successfully invaded. In the early 1860s a Bhutan feudal-type lord of the Punakha province rebuked Britain’s offer of a peace treaty and as a result Britain declared war on Bhutan. The following war lasted only 5 months but the result was a treaty between the two, some border lands returned to India, and a per annum to Bhutan of 50,000 rupees.

The following decade lead to another civil war, which ended with Ugyen Wangchuck being made governor of one of the provinces. In 1907 Wangchuck was enthroned as hereditary king of Bhutan, which was immediately recognized by Britain. The Wangchuck monarchy existed as an absolute monarchy until the 21st century when the 4th king announced the plan to evolve into a constitutional monarchy. In 2006 after abdicating to his son, the 5th Wangchuck king was crowned, following that in 2008 the Bhutanese general elections were held and a constitution was signed.2
