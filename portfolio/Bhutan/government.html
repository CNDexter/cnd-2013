Like England, Bhutan is a constitutional monarchy. However up until the 21st-Century this was not the case--until 2008 Bhutan was an absolute monarchy. King Jigme Singye Wangchuck, who at that time was the 4th King of Bhutan, initiated the political reformation of the country to a multi-party system with elections.

Bhutan is currently a two party system; which consists of the Bhutan Peace and Prosperity Party and the People’s Democratic party. The current head of state, the king or Druk Gyalpo as it is known in Dzongkha, is Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck son of Jigme Singye Wangchuck. Since the 2008 elections the Prime Minister is Jigme Y. Thinley of the Peace and Prosperity Party.

The parliamentary system is divided into two houses an upper house, The National Council, and a lower house, The National Assembly. The upper house is made up of 25 individuals who have no political party affiliation; 20 by election, 5 by royal nomination each sitting for a four-year tenure. This house is the more constrained of the two, not being able to author or approve bills pertaining to financial means. Members of the lower house are by election, with a maximum of 55 seated. The 2008 election resulted in 47 seated; 45 from the Peace and Prosperity Party, 2 from the People’s Democratic Party. The lower house is elected by popular vote and has tenure of 5 years.1
